Brand Persona
Glowforge brand positioning:
Our product gives people the ability to make beautiful, useful things without
requiring specialized skills or equipment. Glowforge provides both tools and
technique, letting unskilled users express themselves and advanced users augment
their existing skill base.
Tone of voice:
Glowforge is truly interested in connecting people to creative solutions and is that
smart, energetic and approachable person that you want to talk to at a party or
gathering. Glowforge is knowledgeable and ready to offer advice. Glowforge is
excited to learn about what you’re creating, and encourages you to have fun
experimenting.
Every message we put into the world should leave the reader inspired to explore
where their ideas will take them next and with the confidence to tackle a new
project. Our goal is to make using a 3D laser printer sound attainable and inviting,
never overly technical or geeky. We want to make sure each Glowforge owner has
the best possible experience with their Glowforge.
Brand personality:
● Smart
Insightful, helpful, clever, informed
● Trustworthy
Honest, responsible, consistent
● Genuine
Refreshing, mindful, sincere
● Likeable
Relatable, inviting, charismatic

What do we stand for?
● Homemade is better/call for a renaissance of homemade
● Lasers today are expensive and hard to use/access—let’s change that
○ “If you wanted to make something before the Industrial Revolution, you
found the materials to make it—and the result was durable, beautiful,
and exactly what you wanted,” Glowforge co-founder and CEO Dan
Shapiro says. “We gave that up when we went to factories. I want to
reinvent the notion of homemade and put the factory on the desktop.”

● Anyone can create

Guiding Principles
Writing Strategies
● Be easy-to-digest, not verbose, boastful, or overly flowery
● Descriptive and specific versus abstract or superlative: intricate card vs.
beautiful card; warm leather vs. superior leather; tend not to use “best”, “very”,
“wonderful”
● Use active voice
○ Instead of “Your Glowforge can be used to create thoughtful gifts,” say
“Use your Glowforge to create thoughtful gifts”
● First and second-person language (“we” and “you”)
● Avoid cliches and overused idioms (“cutting edge” and “outside the box”)
● Avoid puns
● Break the fourth wall to establish rapport with readers (“We’re sure you
remember referring friends to the Glowforge launch list...”)
● Talk about things in this order: 1) The project: materials, design, aesthetics; 2)
The owner/project creator: inspiration, background; 3) Glowforge and its
features
● Tone will be slightly different depending on the medium (blog, social media
posts, crowdfund campaign updates, email drips and campaigns, etc.)

○ For each circumstance of tone, it’s important to consider the reader’s
state of mind and adjust accordingly. Examples:
■ Excited about the announcement of a pre-order campaign
■ Disappointed that there’s been a delay in shipping

Audience Persona
● What baseline knowledge and abilities should we assume about our
audience?
○ Moderately tech-savvy (daily use of computer/smartphone for work and
personal life)
○ They have heard of 3D printing and have a vague idea of what it is
○ The purchaser is an adult (although teens and kids may influence the
decision)

● What can’t we take for granted?
○ That they know anything about lasers
○ That they consider themselves “makers”, “designers”, “artists”
○ That they consider themselves creative, technical, capable of inventing
things, “handy”
○ That they have the disposable income to afford the product (it may be a
major decision they have to make sacrifices to afford)

Dan Voice
Writing Strategies
● Pay particular attention to applying Influence principles whenever possible
● Tone is more humble, sincere, and enthusiastic that regular Glowforge voice
● Likes short sentences, especially symmetrical ones that reflect on each other
● Uses “we” when speaking on behalf of the company only, use “I” for personal
statements
● Speaks directly to customer
○ Example: not “if you make a US order” but “if you’re in the US”
● Uses more colloquial phrases
○ Example: not “is really challenging to use” but “is a giant pain in the
neck to use”

● Is willing to take blame in order to get the feeling of total honesty and
transparency across
● Uses specific and anecdotal evidence whenever possible
○ Example: Instead of “Wow! We’ve reached 10,000 backers!” will tell the
story of discovering he’d reached 10,000 backers while out to coffee
with his daughter, and how he explained the volume of people to her
● When organization information, start with a simple summarizing statement
up top, and then elaborate
● Voice is self-deprecating at times - not overly so
○ Example: “Well, maybe you can draw a pendant... I’m a terrible artist.
But I can trace a quarter, and draw a smiley face in the middle, and
make my kids’ day with it. Real artists can do a lot more.”

● Uses more fun, wholesome phrases
○ Example: “Thanks a million!” “Folks” “Heck, the campaign is only 40
hours old”
Forum Announcements
● If not posting at the start of the month, make a note of it
● Summarize contents of entire update in bullet points at the start of the post
● Be mindful of our customer state of mind
○ Example: Our customers tend to get mad when we tell them that we’re
growing the business (why are you doing that when you haven't done
the thing I wanted?) or selling to more customers (same thing)
● Any time we talk about deliveries, mention some version of: “If you
pre-ordered a unit and are in the US or EU, and haven’t heard that your unit is
ready, your email got lost! Contact us at support@glowforge.com.”
● When talking about updates, have them come from the customer:

○ Example: “We’ve heard you tell us that you wish it could be easier to
purchase a Glowforge...”
App Text and Error Messages
Writing app text and error messages is about transitioning from technical
language to friendly language, but it’s about more than that. It’s about going
from language that conveys
100% of the information in a way that 20% of readers understand,
to conveying
80% of the information in a way that 80% of the readers understand.
Writing Strategies
● Use simple words. Avoid jargon or technical terms. Write for our least
sophisticated user.
● Don’t need to use TM or ®.
● Don’t be funny or familiar; be simple and reassuring.
● Describe the problem in the way the user thinks of it, not the way the software
thinks of it. Describe what they can do about it.
● When talking about a design, say “this design”, as in “this design has an error”.
Don’t say “Your design” or “the design”.
● Use “printer” (not machine, hardware, etc)
● “Your printer” but “the print” (“Your printer is busy” / “The print is too big”)
● Make the title (the short headline, not the body) a simple declaration of what
happened. (“Print too big”, “Snapmarks didn’t align”)
● The goal is the briefest, clearest way to convey the sense of what is happening
(“Focusing”), not a fully correct statement of the world (“Measuring distance
from head to material for laser focus and preview dewarp”).
● Things to avoid:
○ No apologies (“Sorry”)
○ No first person (“We”, “Our”)
○ No accusatory/blame (“You uploaded the wrong...”)
○ No periods at the end of titles.

Examples
No: Action invalidated due to machine re-connect.
Yes: The print has been cancelled because of a network connection problem.
No: The machine is busy performing an action. Please wait for it to complete before
trying again.
Yes: Your printer is busy; try again in a minute.
No: Try rebooting with the head of the Glowforge under the lid camera.
Yes: Turn your printer off, slide the head to the center, and turn it on again.

No: Your print has components partially outside the printable bed. Move your print
away from edges and try again.
Yes: The print is too close to the edge. Move it closer to the center.
Style & Grammar
● What do you do with a Glowforge?
○ You print things, make things, create things.
○ Use “print” rather than engrave or cut in all cases
■ What are these “things”?
● Things, creations, products, projects (use ‘projects’
sparingly, this makes it seem like WORK)

● How do we talk about Glowforge?
○ Your Glowforge
○ Glowforge, the 3D laser printer
○ “The camera on your Glowforge”
● Do we make things “on a Glowforge” or “with a Glowforge”?
○ On a Glowforge
● What do we call our users?
○ We talk about them in the way we want them to think about
themselves in the context of that conversation.

● Example: If we're telling them their machine is shipping
late, we call them "customers" to emphasize the
transaction as completed and emphasize that it’s a ‘done
deal’, and so as not to antagonize them by e.g. calling
them a name they don't identify with ('glowfolk').
■ “Owners” is often a good choice, even though it technically
excludes customers who use another person’s Glowforge
■ When we're talking about folks on the forum gathering together
to see something, we use 'glowfolk' to emphasize community.
■ Use “makers” sparingly
■ Avoid “users”

● Is a Glowforge a printer, machine, device, unit?
○ Printer or 3D laser printer for marketing communications
○ Machine, unit, etc may be appropriate for technical documents like our
manual

● When referring to Glowforge... is it your Glowforge, a Glowforge, the
Glowforge, or just Glowforge?
○ We like “your Glowforge” (the printer) and “Glowforge” (the company) a
lot. Avoid “the Glowforge”..

● Do we pluralize, as “Glowforges will be available next month”
○ No, there should always be a plural noun after Glowforge if we’re talking
about multiplies. Glowforge printers, Glowforge shipments, etc.

● Do we ever refer to Glowforge as just “your laser”? No.
● Verbiage: designs, templates, projects
● Oxford Comma - absolutely, definitely, and yes.
● Exclamation points - for Glowforge voice, use sparingly. For Dan or Bailey
voice, a bit more. Avoid exclamation points on Glowforge.com homepage.
● Infographics - yes
● Linkbait Language - use when you can make it work with the content (ie - “10
holidays gifts you can make in 10 minutes or less” is good)
● Do not use “fire” when talking about the laser (e.g. the laser fires) - same for
any other fire words like “burns”
● Do not use “you’ll get cash” phrasing
● Do not use “function” (e.g. “trace function”) - too technical

Writing Sample
Glowforge voice: long-form writing about the brand
Glowforge: the 3D laser printer.
Let’s face it: mass-manufactured goods lack personality. After all, when’s the last
time you felt inspired by an Amazon.com gift card or an American Apparel t-shirt?

Homemade items, on the other hand, are one-of-a-kind. They’re made with
materials you choose. They’re sized to fit your exact specifications. They’re
customized with your favorite designs or trademark font. They’re just all-around
better.
At Glowforge, we want to help you reclaim your capacity to create beautiful and
functional items that you can use, keep, and cherish.
Alas, you might lament, I don’t have the time/resources/expertise to make things
myself. I don’t have a home woodshop, a heavy-duty sewing machine, or a closet full
of high-end scrapbooking materials.
Neither do we.
Lucky for us, many talents of highly-skilled artisans - leatherworkers, woodsmiths,
paper artists, robotics engineers - can be harnessed with a laser beam. Besides the
ability to produce intricate, perfect results each and every time, precision, lasers
allow us to break free from the plastic aesthetic of traditional 3D-printing. Carve,
sculpt, etch, or engrave on dozens of organic or synthetic materials.
The caveat? Lasers can be inconvenient to access. They’re costly - tens of thousands
of dollars to own or a couple hundred for hourly use - and they require more than a
little computer-savvy (and patience) to operate.
But what if we put a laser on your desktop?
It’s time to forgo the arduous adjustments, finicky focus, and wasted materials. By
taking the guesswork out of laser-craft, you can spend more time being the creative
genius you are.
